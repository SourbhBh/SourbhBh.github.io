Locally Recoverable Codes (LRCs) with Availability (Master's Thesis)

Guide : Prof. Andrew Thangaraj

Modern distributed storage systems use erasure codes to protect themselves against server failures or node failures more efficiently and reliably.
Locally Recoverable Codes (LRCs) are a class of codes that seek to minimize the number of nodes required to repair a failed node. LRCs with
multiple disjoint repair groups find applications in recovering from multiple node failures and accessibility of “hot” data, leading to these codes
being called LRCs with availability.

My work attempts to construct LRCs with availability that are optimal with respect to distance. In addition, I also try to develop upper bounds
for various scenarios concerning LRCs with availability and seek to construct codes that meet these bounds. Our key contributions so far include
showing optimality of a known construction for special cases, deriving distance upper bounds for LRCs with unequal locality and availability and
an explicit optimal parity-check matrix construction for LRCs with information locality and availability. Refer Publication 1

Upper Bounds for Gaussian MIMO Z Channels and Weighted Sum-rate Maximization for Gaussian MIMO MAC

Guide : Prof. Srikrishna Bhashyam

I worked with Prof. Srikrishna Bhashyam on two problems in network information theory. The first problem was to find optimal covariance
matrices that maximize the weighted sum-rate for the Gaussian MIMO Multiple Access Channel. For maximizing the sum-rate an elegant
water-filling algorithm is given here. Our approach was to extend these ideas and exploit geometric properties of the existing algorithm to
find a simple waterfilling algorithm that finds covariance matrices which maximize the weighted sum-rate.
The second problem that I worked on was related to Gaussian MIMO Z Channels or the one-sided interference channel. An upper bound
on the sum-rate of the Gaussian MIMO Z Channel was proposed by Jafar and Shamai. Prasad et al proposed a new upper bound based on
reducing the interference at one of the receivers. Numerically, this was shown to be tighter than the previous upper bound. My work focused
on obtaining an analytical proof of the tightness. We showed the equivalence of this problem to a simple comparison of determinants of two
matrices using convex optimization techniques.
